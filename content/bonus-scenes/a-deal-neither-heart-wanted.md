Some storms didn't announce themselves. They simply walked in through the front door, settled into the living room like an unwanted guest, and slowly made the house forget what laughter sounded like.

The Raichura house still looked the same from the outside. A small cream coloured two storeyed house tucked inside a peaceful society in Ahmedabad. The bougainvillea climbing along the compound wall still bloomed every winter. Shilpa still watered her tulsi plant every morning. Nitya still rushed around searching for her school socks before the bus honked outside. Everything looked painfully normal.

Until someone stepped inside. The walls had changed. Not their colour. Their silence...

There was a time when Kaushik Raichura's laughter reached every corner of the house.
He wasn't the richest businessman in Ahmedabad. Neither did he own skyscrapers nor luxury cars lined outside his gate.

But he was rich where it mattered. A wife who believed in him. Two daughters who adored him. A business he had built brick by brick over fifteen years. Every evening he returned home carrying something. Sometimes hot jalebis for Aadhya and Nitya. Sometimes flowers for Shilpa. Sometimes tiny chocolates hidden inside his pocket because Nitya would come running to search him like airport security.

And Aadhya...

She never searched. Because her father always kept her favourite dairy milk on the dashboard of his car.

"It's for my event planner," he would grin. One day...

"Events with Aadhya." He used to say those words as if they already existed, "When your company becomes famous, don't forget your old father."

She would laugh every single time, "I'll make you my permanent VIP guest."

"Free entry?"

"Never."

"My own father has to pay."

"Arrey wah! Looting your own father?"

"Business is business, Mr. Kaushik Raichura."

Those conversations lived in photo frames now.
Only memories knew how warm they once were.

The phone rang at exactly 4:17 in the evening.
It was an ordinary thursday. Aadhya was attending an online certification class while simultaneously sketching decoration layouts for a Nitya's school cultural fest she hoped to organise someday. Shilpa was preparing tea.
Nitya was doing homework while humming an advertisement jingle.

The ringtone cut through the house. Shilpa answered casually. Five seconds later, the steel glass slipped from her hand. It hit the floor with a sharp clang.

"A... accident?" Her voice cracked. Everything froze.

Aadhya looked up instantly, "Mummy?" Shilpa's face had already turned white.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

Hospitals had a strange smell, mixed of sanitizer, medicines. Walls cornered with fear of losing loved ones. The crowded corridors, rushing doctors, stretchers rolling past. Relatives whispering prayers they hadn't remembered in years.

Aadhya ran through the hallway with her bag slipping off one shoulder, desperately searching for the emergency ward. She spotted him. Not her father.

His shoes.

One shoe lay near the entrance, stained with dust and blood. Her chest tightened.

A minute later, the doctor stepped outside, "Family of Mr. Kaushik Raichura?"

All three stood together.

"He's alive." The sentence should have brought relief. Instead, "...but..." No family ever liked hearing that word. "The impact crushed his lower spine. There is severe damage to his left leg. We'll try everything we can..." Another pause. "...but the chances of him walking again are extremely low."

Everything blurred. The ceiling, the walls, the doctor's moving figure. 

Aadhya heard words like paralysis, spinal injury, emergency surgery, long rehabilitation, permanent disability. Each one landed heavier than the last.

Beside her, Nitya quietly tugged her sleeve, "Didi..." Her voice trembled. "Papa will come home... right?"

Aadhya opened her mouth. Nothing came out. For the first time in her life...

She didn't have an answer.

The surgery lasted eight hours. Eight hours of staring at the red operation theatre light. Eight hours of pretending hope was enough.
Eight hours where every passing second felt borrowed.

When Kaushik finally opened his eyes the next morning, the first thing he did wasn't ask about himself. He looked at Shilpa, "Aadhu, Nitu?"

"They're okay."

Kaushik's accident didn't just take his leg. It took the floor out from under an entire household. One day he was Kaushik Raichura, respected businessman, family's head, the man who used to complain about traffic and cricket scores. The next, he was a man in a wheelchair learning how to exist in a body that no longer listened to him, staring at the ceiling of a private room that was quietly bankrupting his family, one invoice at a time.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

Recovery didn't come with celebrations. It came with bills. One after another.

Room charges, medicines, ICU expenses, specialists, physiotherapy, wheelchair, home modifications, monthly treatments.

Every signature Shilpa placed on another hospital paper felt like another piece of their savings disappearing forever. Within weeks...

Years of careful planning vanished.

Fixed deposits. Gone.
Savings. Gone.
Investments. Gone.
The family car. Sold.

A small plot Kaushik had once bought hoping to build a warehouse. Sold.

Every asset that could breathe, was sold.

The hospital discharged Kaushik after weeks.
He came home. Not walking but rolling in a wheelchair. The wheelchair crossed the entrance first. Shilpa stood holding the aarti thali, trying to smile through eyes swollen from sleepless nights. Kaushik lowered his gaze. He couldn't even step into his own house. His wife had to push him inside. That pain...

No medicine could numb it.

Then came the visitors. Some arrived carrying fruits. Some carried sympathy. Most carried curiosity.

"God tests only good people."

"We're praying."

"Everything happens for a reason."

"Stay strong."

The words sounded comforting. Until they left. Then began the phone calls.

"The business won't survive now."

"Who's running it?"

"They should sell everything."

"I heard the company is drowning."

"Poor daughters..."

Aadhya heard every single sentence. People thought walls couldn't listen. They forgot daughters existed inside those walls. One afternoon she found her mother sitting alone.

Her mind drifted to relatives. The same ones who used to compliment Shilpa's saris at every function, who used to boast to neighbors about "our Kaushik bhai's success" ...they disappeared the moment the word loan entered the conversation. Phone calls stopped being returned. Invitations quietly stopped coming.

Shilpa never cried in front of anyone. Not once. She sold her jewelry piece by piece — the mangalsutra's backup chain first, then the bangles she wore only on Diwali, then finally the necklace her own mother had given her, the one she always said she'd pass down to Aadhya's someday-wedding. Nobody asked her how it felt. She didn't offer to say.

She was simply staring at an empty jewellery box. The same box that once held wedding bangles, gold earrings, necklaces gifted during festivals. Every piece had a memory attached.

Her wedding.
Her first anniversary.
Aadhya's birth.
Nitya's naming ceremony.

One by one... Those memories had been exchanged across a jeweller's counter for hospital bills. No one stopped her. Shilpa simply removed each ornament with trembling fingers. Signed the receipt. Folded the cash carefully.
And came home carrying an empty velvet box.

That evening, she still made everyone's favourite **_malpua_** (A Gujarati dish, sweet pancakes made up of wheat flour, jaggery and ghee). As if nothing had happened. Aadhya watched her from the kitchen doorway.
That was the day she truly understood something. Mothers had a terrifying superpower. They broke silently. So their children wouldn't have to.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

The next few months changed Aadhya more than the previous twenty-four years ever had. Dreams quietly stepped aside. Responsibilities took their place.

There's a version of Aadhya Raichura that only exists in old photographs. The one laughing at her cousin's marriage with mehendi still fresh on her palms. The one who stayed up till 3 AM sketching mood boards for weddings she dreamed of planning one day, whispering 'Events with Aadhya' to herself like it was a spell that would eventually come true if she said it enough times.

That version died quietly, somewhere between a hospital corridor and a stack of unpaid bills.

She looked at the notebook where she had written in neat handwriting years ago.

_Events with Aadhya_.
A logo.
Business plans.
Venue contacts.
Decoration sketches.
Colour palettes.
Marketing ideas.
Everything she had plan to built over years.

She closed the notebook. Placed it inside the cupboard. Not because she stopped believing in it. Because dreams didn't pay electricity bills. They didn't buy medicines. They didn't pay school fees.

Reality did.

And reality demanded sacrifice. So she stopped waiting for opportunities. She started chasing survival. 

She accepted a full-time job as an audit officer because it paid consistently.
In the evenings she designed invitations, branding kits and digital creatives for a startup that barely remembered to appreciate her work.

After dinner, while the rest of Ahmedabad slept, she connected her headset and taught students online until two or sometimes three in the morning, her eyes burning from exhaustion as different time zones dictated her schedule.

Three jobs. One house to hold up. A sister's school fees. A father's medical expense. A mother pretending her hands didn't ache from hours behind a sewing machine. And somewhere underneath all of it, a version of Aadhya who used to laugh easily, slowly forgetting what that even felt like.

A few relatives so called well wishers, the truly generous ones, showed up only to remind Aadhya, with syrupy concern dripping from every syllable, exactly how far the family had fallen. "Beta, at least you have your job, na? Some families have nothing left at all." As if that was comfort. As if that wasn't a knife dressed up as kindness.

Nobody talks enough about what happens to the eldest daughter in moments like these.

She wasn't allowed to be tired. Eldest daughters don't get to be tired, they get to be the example. The standard. The one who has to be okay, loudly and visibly okay, so the rest of the family has permission to fall apart a little, knowing someone's still holding the roof up.

Her weekdays blurred into weekends. Monday looked like Friday. Friday looked like Sunday.
Dates lost meaning. Sleep became a luxury. Coffee became routine. Smiles became rehearsed.

Yet every morning before leaving, she would peek into her parents' room. Her father, once the strongest person she knew, now struggled to move from his bed to the wheelchair without help. Her mother measured medicines with shivering hands, pretending she wasn't counting every rupee left in the kitchen drawer.
Nitya still laughed, still talked about school competitions, still dreamed like children should.
And that alone reminded Aadhya why she couldn't afford to fall apart.

People often romanticised being the eldest child. They called them...

Responsible. Mature. Dependable. 

The pride of the family. What they rarely spoke about was the invisible weight that title carried. The eldest child was expected to become an adult before they were ready. To understand financial stress without being told.
To notice when groceries needed replacing before anyone asked. To smile first so younger siblings wouldn't worry. 

To sacrifice first because they were "understanding." 

To postpone dreams because "you're mature enough." 

To hear, "You're the elder one, adjust," so often that adjusting became second nature.

Nobody ever asked who the eldest child adjusted for. They became the emergency contact. The unpaid therapist. The backup parent. The problem solver.

The silent worrier lying awake at three in the morning calculating expenses that weren't even theirs. They learned to celebrate everyone else's achievements while quietly shelving their own. 

Not because they didn't have ambitions. But because love sometimes looked like choosing your family over yourself, again and again, until you forgot what choosing yourself even felt like.

Nobody tells them that being the eldest means their dreams get filed under "later" the second the family needs a solution, and the family always needs a solution. Nobody tells them that people will look at their exhaustion and call it maturity, will look at their sacrifice and call it duty, like they were always meant to give this much, like wanting something for themselves was ever the indulgence and not the birthright everyone else got to keep.

Aadhya never complained. Not because it didn't hurt. Because she feared if she started speaking, she might never stop. So she kept moving.

One step. One shift. One paycheck. One responsibility at a time.

Months passed. The house slowly found its rhythm again. The medicine cabinet stayed full. Nitya's school fees were paid on time. The electricity bill no longer caused panic.

There were even evenings when Shilpa laughed at a television serial and Kaushik smiled without guilt. For the first time in months, it felt like maybe...

Just maybe...

The worst had finally passed. Aadhya allowed herself that fragile hope. She didn't know fate had only been catching its breath.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

Hope was a dangerous thing. It made people believe they had finally reached the shore, only for another wave to pull them back into the sea.

The Raichura family had learned to live differently. Not happily enough.

Just... differently.

Kaushik had become an expert at moving around the house in his wheelchair without asking for help. Every morning, he still insisted on reading the newspaper at the dining table, pretending life hadn't changed much.

Shilpa had transformed one corner of the living room into her tiny tailoring space. A second-hand sewing machine sat beside the window, its rhythmic sound becoming the heartbeat of the house. Saree falls, blouse alterations, school uniforms. She accepted every order that came her way.

Nitya had stopped asking for expensive birthday gifts. Instead, she would quietly whisper, "This one is enough, Mummy." Children understood far more than adults believed.

And Aadhya...

She had become a stranger to herself. Dark circles had settled permanently beneath her eyes. The sparkle that once appeared whenever she talked about event themes or wedding decorations had faded into something quieter. She still smiled. 

Just not with her eyes anymore.

One Saturday afternoon, after returning from her audit job, she unlocked the front door with tired hands.

"Didi..." Nitya came running, "A letter came."

She handed over a thick brown envelope with no sender's name. Only a company logo embossed in black.

Aadhya frowned. She didn't recognize it. She placed her office bag on the sofa and casually tore open the envelope. The first page slid out.
Her eyes stopped on one line.

**_FINAL NOTICE BEFORE LEGAL RECOVERY._**

She frowned harder. Maybe it had been delivered to the wrong address. Then she saw her father's name.

Mr. Kaushik Raichura.

Her heartbeat slowed. She continued reading. Every line made less sense than the previous one with loan Account Number, recovery Proceedings, outstanding Amount, penalty Interest, final Deadline. The mail that undid all of peace in a single paragraph.

Ten crore rupees.

Aadhya read it twice, certain it was a clerical error. It wasn't. She blinked once. Twice. Three times. The number refused to change. The paper slipped from her fingers. It floated onto the floor.

Nitya picked it up innocently, "Didi?"

Aadhya snatched it back before she could read, "Go... go finish your homework."

Her voice sounded unfamiliar. Nitya nodded and walked away. The moment she disappeared upstairs, Aadhya turned toward the study. Her father was there. Looking out the window. His wheelchair faced the garden.

She entered without knocking, "Papa."

Kaushik turned. One look at the paper in her trembling hands and the colour drained from his face. He knew. He had always known this day would come.

"You knew?" Her voice barely rose above a whisper, "You knew about this?" She stepped closer, "Tell me."

The silence hurt more than any answer could.

Finally, Kaushik closed his eyes, "Sit, beta."

"I don't want to sit."

His fingers tightened around the wheelchair armrest, "Aadhya..."

"No, tell me please."

The room became unbearably quiet. Outside, someone in the neighbourhood laughed.
Inside, a family was about to fall apart all over again.

Kaushik inhaled slowly. His confession came out in broken pieces, shame thick in every word. The business had been bleeding for months before the accident... clients leaving, workers quitting, the kind of quiet collapse that a proud man hides until he physically cannot anymore. Every bank had turned him away; there was nothing left to leverage. Friends helped where they could, but friendship has a ceiling. And then one friend, meaning well or not, had passed along a contact — a fixer for some overseas-based entrepreneur looking to plant roots in Indian business through local allies.

Kaushik had asked for five lakhs. Just enough to keep the lights on, keep the workers paid, buy a few more months.

What he signed... buried under clauses he didn't read closely enough, worded by people who understood exactly what they were doing, was a loan of ten crores. Money that never once touched his hands. A number that existed only on paper and now existed as a debt clock ticking down toward a deadline.

"The one who introduced them to me, claimed he knew nothing."

She looked at him in disbelief, "You filed a complaint?"

"I was preparing one, beta. I collected documents. I even met a lawyer. I was driving back that day..." He stopped.

That day. The accident. Everything connected.

The complaint was never filed. The case was never fought.

Life had simply... moved on without asking.
Tears silently rolled down Kaushik's cheeks, "I failed as a father, as a husband."

"No Papa."

"I failed all of you."

"Papa..."

"I couldn't save the business. I couldn't protect this family. I couldn't even read a contract properly." He looked at his own motionless legs.
"And now I can't even stand."

Those words shattered something inside Aadhya. She had never seen her father this broken. The man who once carried both daughters on his shoulders now believed he had become a burden.

She knelt beside his wheelchair. Not because he needed comfort. Because she did. She gently held his hand. It felt older. Rougher. Heavier.

"You didn't fail us."

"I did."

"No."

"If I had read every page... If I had questioned them... If I had..." She squeezed his hand. 

"If." Such a cruel word. It always arrived after everything was already over.

"You trusted people. That wasn't your mistake. The mistake was theirs." 

He looked away, "What difference does that make? The notice doesn't care." The envelope still lay on the table. Like a ticking clock, "The deadline..."

Kaushik whispered, "...is in three weeks."

Three weeks. Twenty-one days. Twenty-one days to arrange ten crores. Even saying the amount sounded absurd. They didn't have ten lakh. Forget ten crores. The surgery bills still lingered. Physiotherapy payments were due. The tailoring income barely covered groceries.
Her salaries kept the household alive, not wealthy.

Yet the paper demanded an amount that could swallow generations. That night, no one slept except Nitya.

Aadhya sat with that information the way you sit with news that rearranges your entire nervous system. Surgery was scheduled. Recovery was scheduled. Nothing else was. The deadline for repayment didn't care about any of it.

She spent days making calls across Ahmedabad, calling in every favor, every connection, every acquaintance who might know someone who knew someone. Doors closed one after another, politely or not.

She had always believed hard work solved problems. This time...

Hard work wasn't enough. Some problems demanded power. Power she didn't have. One evening, after another rejection, she drove without knowing where she was going. The roads eventually led her somewhere familiar.

Sabarmati Riverfront.

She parked her scooty and walked to the stone steps overlooking the quietly flowing river. The sunset painted the water in shades of orange and gold. Families laughed nearby. Children chased pigeons. Couples clicked photographs.

The world continued as if hers wasn't collapsing.

She sat alone. The envelope rested beside her. She stared at the river for what felt like hours.
For the first time in years, she allowed herself to cry.

Just silent tears that carried months of exhaustion, "_Shu karis have hu?"_
(What do I do now...?)

The wind answered with silence. She closed her eyes. And in that stillness, without her permission, one name surfaced. Not chosen. Not searched for. Just there, the way a name is there when it's been living rent-free in someone's chest for years.

Nisarg Panchal.

She opened her eyes instantly, "No..."

She shook her head, "Not him."

But the name refused to leave. Her mind rejected it. Her heart repeated it.

Again.
Again.
Again.

She hadn't seen him in years. Hadn't let herself think his name in years, not really, not on purpose. The last real memory she had of him was public and humiliating... her own heart on full display, and his rejection just as public, just as final.

The man who now stood among the most influential lawyers in Gujarat. The man whose surname alone opened doors.

Panchal.

She knew, distantly, the way you know things about a life you're no longer part of, that he'd done exactly what he said he would. Chosen law over the family business despite what everyone expected of a Panchal. Become the name every legal column couldn't stop writing about. Won the cases that made headlines. Built the kind of reputation that made people say his name a little differently.

Power. Confidence. An ego people whispered about behind his back, and an arrogance that apparently only grew sharper with success.

She hated that her only hope wore that name. She hated even more that somewhere deep inside...

Her heart still remembered his smile.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

The city had fallen asleep. Ahmedabad's roads, usually alive with endless traffic, were quieter now. Streetlights stretched across the Sabarmati River like broken pieces of the moon, trembling with every ripple of water.

Aadhya sat motionless on the stone steps. The legal notice rested on her lap. The words ten crores had become etched into her mind.

Ten crores. It wasn't just a number. It was a mountain. And she stood at its foot with nothing but determination and a family waiting behind her. She leaned forward, resting her elbows on her knees.

"Nisarg Panchal." The name escaped her lips before she could stop it. She hated how familiar it still felt.

Six years had passed. 

Years. 

People were supposed to move on. They were supposed to forget. She had tried. God knew she had tried. She had drowned herself in work, responsibilities, deadlines and sleepless nights. Yet one name still found its way back whenever her heart became too quiet.

She still remembered the first day she had seen him. Their university's Law department's annual inter-university debate. Everyone else had come prepared with speeches. He had walked onto the stage with nothing but confidence.

He dismantled every argument presented against him so calmly that people didn't realize they had already lost until he finished speaking.

Cocky. Arrogant. Brilliant.

He spoke as though the entire courtroom already belonged to him. She had hated him. For exactly seven minutes. After that...

She simply couldn't stop looking at him.

Their friendship had never been ordinary. They argued, competed. Mocked each other relentlessly. If someone saw them together, they'd assume they couldn't tolerate one another.

Only they knew every argument somehow ended with coffee. He teased her event plans. She mocked his obsession with legal books. He called decorations unnecessary expenses. She reminded him lawyers charged thousands just to argue over commas. He laughed. She laughed louder. Slowly...

Without either of them realizing, friendship had quietly crossed into something neither dared to name. At least...

She _did._

She was the brave one. Or perhaps the foolish one. She had confessed. 

The entire campus seemed to pause.

Nisarg looked at her expressionless, _"I don't feel the same_."

Her smile had stayed on her face. Until she reached home. Then she cried into her pillow until sunrise.

After that, life happened. She focused on work. He disappeared into law school, internships and eventually headlines. They never met again. 

Her phone vibrated. A notification from a news application. She almost ignored it. Then she noticed the photograph.

**_Senior Advocate Nisarg Panchal Wins High-Profile Corporate Fraud Case_**.

His picture filled the screen in a white shirt, dark charcoal suit, navy tie. Sharp jawline. The same unreadable eyes. Except...

They looked older now. More experienced. More dangerous.

She opened the article. Every paragraph screamed success. Youngest litigation specialist to secure consecutive victories. Corporate legal advisor. Media's favorite legal mind. The Panchal heir who chose courtrooms over boardrooms.

She smiled faintly, "You actually did it..."

He had followed his dream. Exactly as he always said he would. While everyone expected him to join Panchal Corporations...

He had built a name completely separate from it. The entire state now knew him. Not because of his surname. Because of his victories.

She felt proud.

Then immediately hated herself for feeling proud, "No."

She locked her phone, "I can't go."

Every rational part of her screamed that this was a terrible idea. Self-respect has a long memory, and hers remembered every detail of the last time she'd made herself vulnerable in front of Nisarg Panchal.

But being rational didn't pay ten crore rupees. And she had exhausted every version of herself that came before the version willing to walk back into that man's life.

She stood to leave. Took three steps. Stopped. Looked back at the river. Then imagined home.
Her father. Trying to hide the legal notice beneath newspapers. Her mother calculating expenses with trembling fingers. Nitya studying for exams, unaware the roof over her head could disappear. Suddenly...

Her pride felt incredibly small. Sometimes life cornered people so badly that dignity became a luxury.

She closed her eyes, "I'm sorry..."

She didn't know whether she was apologizing to herself or to the younger version of herself who once promised never to stand before Nisarg Panchal again.

The next morning began with research. Unlike college days, finding someone like Nisarg wasn't difficult anymore. His achievements flooded every search result.

News articles.
Interviews.
Legal seminars.
Corporate conferences.
Awards.
Magazine covers.
Every article called him fearless. Untouchable. 

One article caught her attention.

Appointments by prior approval only. High-profile corporate and criminal litigation. She exhaled slowly.

Of course. A lawyer like him wouldn't be easy to meet.

She opened his firm's website. The calendar was booked for weeks. Her shoulders slumped. Then she noticed one line. Urgent confidential matters may request priority review.

An idea formed. She didn't like it. But desperate people rarely had the privilege of liking their decisions.

Three phone calls and two emails. One carefully worded request. She never mentioned her own name. Instead, she framed as an urgent, high-profile legal matter... the kind no ambitious lawyer turns away. She talked her way past a receptionist, then his assistant, insisting the details could only go to their senior lawyer, Nisarg Panchal directly. Requiring immediate legal consultation under three important conditions: Strict confidentiality, Time-sensitive, Corporate implications.

Within hours, his firm responded.

Appointment Approved. Tomorrow, 11:30 AM.

She stared at the confirmation email for almost a minute.

It was happening.

That night she barely slept. Not because she was preparing to ask someone for money. Because she was preparing to face the man she never truly stopped loving. She hated admitting it.

Even to herself.

Love didn't always disappear just because it wasn't returned. Sometimes...

It simply learned to stay quiet.

The next morning she stood before her wardrobe for almost twenty minutes. Then laughed at herself.

Why was she thinking about clothes?

She wasn't going to impress him. She was going to beg. Still...

She chose a simple pastel cotton kurta. No makeup except kajal. Hair tied neatly with her favorite matching color scrunchie. A small silver watch. No jewellery.

She looked into the mirror. The girl staring back wasn't the carefree college student Nisarg once knew. She looked...

Older. Tired. Life had carved quiet strength into her features.

She picked up the file containing every loan document, repayment notice, medical records and financial statements.

Before leaving, she glanced toward her father's room. Kaushik was asleep. His wheelchair stood beside the bed. Shilpa adjusted his blanket before noticing Aadhya, "Leaving for office?"

Aadhya forced a smile, "Hmm."

It wasn't entirely a lie. She simply didn't have the courage to tell them where she was really going. Because if this failed...

She wanted to carry the disappointment alone.

By 11:15 AM.

She stood outside one of Ahmedabad's most prestigious corporate towers. A giant silver logo reflected sunlight across the glass facade. Employees in tailored suits walked confidently through the revolving doors. Luxury cars lined the entrance. Security personnel stood alert.

Everything about the building whispered power.

She suddenly became aware of the worn strap of her office bag. The faded corner of her file. The fact that she hadn't bought herself new sandals in over a year. She inhaled deeply. This wasn't about appearances. It was about Papa. 

She stepped inside. The marble floors gleamed beneath crystal lights. The reception area looked more like a five-star hotel than a law firm. 

A young receptionist greeted her professionally, "Good morning, ma'am. Your appointment?"

"An eleven-thirty meeting." She slid the email confirmation across.

The receptionist checked her system, "Name?"

For half a second, Aadhya hesitated. Then calmly repeated the anonymous corporate identity she'd used in the email.

The receptionist nodded, "Please have a seat."

Aadhya sat. Every passing second made her heartbeat louder. She wasn't nervous about asking for money anymore. She was nervous about seeing him.

After years.
After his brutal rejection.
After pretending she had moved on.

She didn't know that somewhere several floors above...

Nisarg Panchal was absentmindedly signing legal papers, completely unaware that the woman who had unknowingly occupied every quiet corner of his heart for years was sitting in his reception area.

And in less than five minutes...

Both of their lives were about to change forever.

The receptionist glanced at her screen before picking up the intercom, "Sir, your eleven-thirty appointment is here."

A brief pause.

Then a deep, composed voice echoed through the receiver, "Send them in."

Aadhya's fingers tightened around the file resting on her lap. Her heartbeat grew louder with every passing second. She slowly stood.

The receptionist smiled politely, "Please take the elevator to the thirty-second floor. Mr. Panchal's assistant will guide you."

Thirty-second floor.

She almost laughed.

Back in college, Nisarg used to joke, "_One day my office will be so high you'll need oxygen to reach me."_

She had thrown a notebook at him, "_You and your impossible dreams."_

Looks like one of them had come true.

The elevator doors opened. The atmosphere changed instantly. The floor was quieter than the lobby. Minimalistic. Elegant. Grey marble. Walnut wood panels. Framed legal recognitions lined the corridor.

A young assistant approached, "Good morning, ma'am. This way please."

As they walked, Aadhya's eyes wandered across the walls.

Photographs. Corporate conferences. Legal seminars. Award ceremonies. In almost every frame...

There he was. Standing with judges. Industrialists. Government officials. His smile remained as rare as she remembered. The assistant stopped before a large frosted glass door.

**NISARG PANCHAL** 

**_Senior Litigation Counsel_**

He knocked twice, "Sir?"

"Come."

The assistant opened the door, "Your client." Without waiting for a response, he stepped aside.

Aadhya entered. The door closed behind her with a soft click.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

The office was... unmistakably Nisarg. Organized to perfection. Floor-to-ceiling bookshelves filled with legal volumes. A large walnut desk. One wall entirely made of glass, overlooking Ahmedabad's skyline. No unnecessary decorations. No flashy awards on display. Just quiet authority.

He stood near the window, his back facing her. One hand rested inside his trouser pocket. The other held a cup of black coffee.

His charcoal suit fit him effortlessly. Broad shoulders. Straight posture. Everything about him radiated confidence. He looked different. Taller in the way success makes a person stand, more filled-out, sharper around the jaw, more finished, somehow, than the boy she remembered.

What she didn't know... what she had no way of knowing, was what those years had actually done to him.

Without turning around, he spoke, "So Ms. Ahuja, what is this confidential matter that couldn't wait?"

His voice had deepened. It carried the same arrogance. Only calmer now. More dangerous.

Aadhya didn't answer. She couldn't. She was too busy staring. Years. It had been years. 

He slowly turned around. His expression remained professionally indifferent. For exactly one second. The coffee cup nearly slipped from his hand.

His entire body froze, "...Aadhya?"

The name escaped him before he could stop it. Neither of them moved. Neither of them blinked. Time, somehow, forgot to exist.

She looked different. The girl who once filled every room with loud laughter had become a woman wrapped in quiet exhaustion. Her cheeks had lost their youthful softness. Dark circles rested beneath her eyes. Her shoulders looked smaller somehow, as though life had been placing invisible weights on them for years. Yet...

She was still breathtaking. Not because she looked perfect. Because she looked real. And somehow...

That made her even more beautiful than the girl he'd fallen in love with.

Nisarg had never stopped cataloguing her. Quietly, obsessively, the way people do with things they've told themselves they're over. He knew which chai stall she used to like near her college. He knew she hummed old Kishore Kumar songs under her breath when she was nervous. He knew the exact shape of her handwriting from a birthday card she probably didn't remember writing him.

Nisarg's heart betrayed him first. It slammed violently against his ribs.

She's here.

Not in a dream. Not in another memory he'd replay before sleeping. She was standing five feet away from him. After all these years. He had imagined this moment countless times.

Sometimes she would yell at him. Sometimes she'd slap him. Sometimes she'd ignore him completely. Never, not once...

Had he imagined she'd stand before him looking so... broken. Something inside him twisted painfully. 

He forced his face back into its usual unreadable expression. He had spent years mastering control. He wouldn't lose it now.

"So..." He leaned casually against the edge of his desk, "What brings you here?" His tone sounded almost bored. Inside, his pulse refused to slow down.

_Aya...

Why do you look like you've been carrying the world alone?

Who did this to you?

Why didn't you come to me sooner?

Have you been eating properly?

Are you sleeping at all?

Why are your hands trembling?_

A thousand questions screamed inside his head. Not one reached his lips. Instead, he smirked, "I assume this isn't a social visit in the name of Ms. whatever Ahuja."

He opened his mouth, because that's what he always did... cocky, a little cutting, the practiced arrogance of a man who liked being the one in control of a room.

She lowered her gaze. Still silent.

His heartbeat grew heavier. He knew her. Better than anyone realized. She only became this quiet when something was terribly wrong. College Aadhya would have already insulted him twice by now. This woman...

Hadn't spoken a single word. The silence frightened him more than tears would have. He took one slow step closer.

"Aadhya." Nothing.

Another step, "What happened?"

Still silence. Then...

Without warning, she bent down. He frowned. For a split second, he didn't understand what she was doing. Until she lowered herself onto her knees. Right in front of him. The file slipped from her hands onto the polished wooden floor.

Not performing anything. Just a woman who had run out of every other option, choosing the last one left, even if it cost her the dignity she'd spent years protecting from this exact man.

He was on his feet before she'd fully bent her knees, pulling her back up, some old instinct in him refusing to let her stay down like that in front of him of all people. And underneath the discomfort of watching her like this... the woman he'd secretly, stubbornly adored even in her absence, something else moved through him. Disgust, but not at her. At himself, for the years he'd spent pretending this feeling had faded.

"Aadhya!" He reached her in two strides, "What are you doing?"

She kept her head lowered, "Please..." Her voice cracked. "...help me."

Something inside him shattered. The woman who had once carried enough pride to confess her feelings in front of an entire college...

The woman who never bowed before anyone...

The woman who argued with professors without hesitation...

Was kneeling. In front of him. For help. He didn't think. He immediately caught both her shoulders. His voice came out sharper than intended, "No." 

He gently but firmly pulled her back onto her feet, "Don't." 

She couldn't look at him. 

He saw the tears she was desperately trying to hide. He saw the exhaustion. The defeat. And he hated himself. Because somewhere deep inside...

A selfish part of him was relieved. Relieved that she had come to him. Not because she needed money. Because after years...

She still knew his name. Still believed, even in desperation, that if there was one person who could help...

It was him. He hated that selfish thought the moment it appeared. She deserved better than someone secretly finding comfort in her pain.

"Aadhya..." His voice softened. For the first time since she entered...

The lawyer disappeared. Only the man remained.

"What do you need?"

Those four words finally broke the wall she'd been holding together. She closed her eyes, inhaled shakily, and placed the thick file on his desk. When she spoke, every sentence felt heavier than the last.

"My father..." She told him everything. She didn't dramatize it. She simply stated facts. Perhaps because she'd cried enough over them already.

Nisarg didn't interrupt once. He opened the file. Read every document. Every notice. Every clause. His jaw clenched tighter with every page. By the time he reached the forged loan agreement...

His eyes had turned cold. Not the cold Aadhya remembered from college. This was dangerous. The courtroom version of Nisarg Panchal. The one newspapers feared. He quietly closed the file, "This is fraud."

She nodded.

"This isn't a valid consent. They manipulated the agreement." His fingers tapped once against the desk, "They're scammers and soon to be finished."

She looked up, "What?"

"The people behind this." His voice remained calm, "I'll bury them legally."

For the first time that day, a tiny flicker of hope appeared in her eyes. She promised repayment, every rupee, on whatever timeline he wanted.

She stood there. Waiting for his answer. Completely unaware...

That the man standing across from her had already made his decision years ago. The day she confessed. The day he lied. He had rejected her in front of everyone, not because he didn't feel it, but because feeling it terrified a boy who had built his entire identity on never needing anyone, least of all a girl who made him feel like his carefully constructed control meant nothing. The day he chose to break his own heart instead of dragging her into the messy world surrounding the Panchals.

He told himself it was for the best. He built a career loud enough to drown out the quiet. He let his name become bigger than his feelings. And now she was standing in his office, silent in a way that unsettled him more than any courtroom cross-examination ever had.

Back then, he had convinced himself letting her go was love. Today...

Fate had walked her right back into his office. And this time, he wasn't letting her leave so easily. He looked at her for a long moment.

Then quietly said, "I'll help you."

Aadhya's eyes widened. Relief washed over her so suddenly that her knees almost gave way again. A thankful smile, small but genuine, appeared on her face.

"Thank you..." She whispered, "You don't know what this means to me."

Nisarg looked at her for a second longer. Then took a slow breath.

"There is..." He paused, "...one condition."

The smile disappeared from her face. She felt her heartbeat quicken once again, "What condition?"

Nisarg met her eyes, "If I'm lending you this money then-"

The room fell into absolute silence. It lingered between them after Aadhya finished speaking and Nisarg stopped mid-way.

Not the comfortable kind. The kind that settled after someone had poured every ounce of strength they had left onto the table.

Nisarg lowered his gaze to the file once more.

Medical reports. Hospital bills. Physiotherapy estimates. Loan notices. Recovery proceedings. Every page told the same story. A family trying to survive.

He closed the file gently. His jaw tightened. This wasn't just negligence. This was a carefully planned fraud.

He knew who was behind this. Carter had preyed on a decent man, manipulated legal documents, trapped him in a debt he never asked for, and disappeared before he could fight back. A muscle twitched in his jaw.

_Cowards_.

His fingers slowly curled into fists. If this case ever reached his courtroom...

He wouldn't merely win. He would ruin every single person involved. His eyes drifted toward Aadhya.

She stood quietly, her fingers tightly intertwined, waiting for his answer. Waiting for hope. Waiting for a miracle. She looked exhausted. Not physically. Soulfully.

As though life had been asking too much of her for far too long. The woman standing before him wasn't the same girl who once argued with professors over meaningless college debates. This woman had forgotten how to breathe without carrying responsibility. And somehow...

That realization hurt him more than it should have.

He looked away.

_Ten crores_.

For the Panchals... It wasn't an amount capable of shaking their empire.

For Aadhya... It was the difference between losing and saving her family.

He could solve this. Right now with just one phone call and his one signature. One transfer. That was all it would take. His finance team wouldn't even question it. Corporate investments larger than this happened every month. 

He could tell them it was part of a confidential settlement. No one would ask another question.

She would cry. Thank him. Promise to repay every rupee. Walk out of this office. And then, she would disappear after their deal  will be over.

Again.

His breathing faltered. His eyes slowly closed.

Again.

The word echoed inside him like an old wound reopening. He had already watched her leave once. The day she confessed. The day he lied.

_"I don't feel the same_."

Even today, those words tasted bitter. The biggest lie he had ever spoken. Not because he enjoyed hurting her. Because loving her had frightened him. Back then, his life wasn't his own.

He was the son of the Panchals. One of Gujarat's most influential business families. His every move attracted attention.

Corporate rivalries.
Political interests.
Media.
Rivals.

People didn't attack the Panchals directly. They attacked the people they loved. He had watched it happen.

Again.
And again.
And again.

Aadhya had been loud. Fearlessly honest. Bright. She deserved a peaceful life. Not one where bodyguards became routine and threats arrived disguised as flowers. So he had chosen cruelty. Because he believed temporary heartbreak would protect her from permanent danger.

He remembered the way her smile disappeared that day. He remembered watching her walk away without looking back. He remembered standing alone after everyone had left.

Punching the college wall until his knuckles bled. His first victory as a lawyer had felt hollow. His first award had felt incomplete. Every achievement carried the same thought.

_I wish she were here to make fun of me._

Years passed since that day.

His mother had spent months parading alliances in front of him that he had no interest in entertaining, women chosen for lineage and business optics, none of them the one person his heart had apparently decided on years ago without his permission. He saw this... her, standing in front of him with nowhere else to go, as the only door that would ever open twice. His mother never understood. 

His brother did. Raghav had stopped asking years ago. He simply waited. As if he knew Nisarg's heart had already made its choice long before anyone else realized.

Nisarg opened his eyes. She was still there. Standing exactly where she'd been.

Patiently. Silently. Trusting him.

A bitter laugh escaped inside his mind.

_Trusting you?

She trusted you once too.

Look how that ended._

He looked at the file again. Then at her. Then back at the file. If he helped her today...

She would leave. Repay him someday. Move on. Find someone worthy. Build the family she always dreamed of.

And he...

He would return to his empty room in huge Panchal mansion. An empty life. Knowing fate had brought her back once...

Only for him to lose her twice. His chest tightened painfully.

No.

Another voice answered immediately.

_What are you thinking? She came here because she's desperate. She needs a lawyer. She needs help. Not another burden._

He rubbed both hands over his face. Disgust settled heavily inside him. _What kind of man even thinks like this?_

She was drowning. And his heart...

His heart was trying to keep her close. He hated himself. Absolutely hated himself.

He walked away from his desk and stopped before the massive glass window overlooking Ahmedabad. Traffic moved far below. People looked tiny. Insignificant. For the first time in years...

The great Nisarg Panchal had absolutely no idea what the right decision was. 

If he helped her unconditionally, he'd lose her. If he didn't help, he'd lose himself.

He stood there for several long minutes. Eyes fixed on the city. Mind nowhere near it. Then...

His mother's voice echoed through his thoughts.

"_Nishu dikra. At least meet this girl once. She's from an excellent family. How long will you keep rejecting everyone? You can't spend your entire life alone._" He closed his eyes.

No. There had only ever been one woman. There would only ever be one. He slowly turned around.

Aadhya immediately straightened. Hope filled her eyes. She thought he had reached a decision.

He had.

Just not the one she expected. He walked toward her. Each step felt heavier than the last. When he finally stood before her, his voice was calm.

"I'll help you. Bu... but if I'm lending you this money then-" The relief on her face was immediate but still on edge. It was as though someone had removed a mountain from her shoulders. Her eyes shimmered.

He nodded once.

She smiled. A genuine smile. One he hadn't seen in years. It nearly broke him. Because he knew what he was about to say would erase it.

She looked at him, confused, "You're sending me money on what condition?"

He held her gaze. If he looked away now...

He'd never say it, "My mother has been trying to get me married." She frowned slightly. "I've refused every proposal."

Silence.

"I don't intend to marry anyone else." She didn't understand where this conversation was going. Neither did she interrupt. 

He inhaled slowly. Every instinct inside him screamed to stop. To simply transfer the money. To let her go. Instead...

His heart won against his judgment, "If I'm putting my name, my money and my legal protection behind you..."

His voice dropped to barely above a whisper.

"...then become my wife. Marry me, Aadhya."

The room froze. Even the air seemed to stop moving. Aadhya stared at him. She searched his face, waiting for the slightest hint of a joke. There wasn't one.

He was serious.

Silence stretched between them. Then something inside her cracked very painfully.

 _Marriage_.

The word had always meant warmth to her.

A home. Trust. Friendship.

Growing old with someone who chose you every day. She had imagined it once.

With him.

Years ago. But now...

The same man stood before her. Discussing it like a legal agreement. Like another clause. Another contract. Another negotiation. She felt something sacred inside her crumble.

Her throat tightened. She looked at the man she had loved through her college years. The man whose rejection she had accepted without resentment. The man she'd secretly admired every time she saw his achievements in the news. And for the first time...

She questioned her own heart.

_How could I have loved someone who measures a relationship in conditions?

How could the same man who once inspired me... place a price on marriage?

He wanted a marriage. A contract, precisely — two years, on paper, dissolved the moment her debt was cleared._

She swallowed hard. She had walked into his office asking for financial help. She hadn't expected to leave questioning everything she had once believed about love.

Something in Aadhya recoiled. Not just at the proposal, but at what it revealed. The man she had once loved enough to humiliate herself in public for was now offering her marriage the way he'd offer a business merger... terms, duration, conditions. It didn't feel like love returning to her. It felt like love wearing a suit and negotiating.

But her situation had no room left for pride. The moment she agreed, Nisarg prepared the draft, laying out every condition with the precision only a lawyer like him could manage. She read every clause twice, carefully weighing each word before giving her approval. Then, after nearly half an hour of waiting, the official final contract was placed in her hands. Even then, she knew she was agreeing to something that would reshape her life in ways far larger than two years and ten crores.

Outside, Ahmedabad continued as usual. Inside that office, two hearts broke for entirely different reasons.

One because he believed this was the only way to keep the woman he loved from slipping away again. The other because she believed the man she loved had just reduced marriage to the cost of ten crores.

Neither of them knew...

That this painful deal would one day become the very foundation of a love neither of them would ever be able to escape.

Aadhya carefully closed the file after signing the final page. She placed the pen on the desk with slow, deliberate movements, as though prolonging the moment would somehow change what had just happened.

The contract was complete.

A marriage reduced to clauses. A relationship measured in signatures. She gathered her documents and stood, "I'll keep my end of the deal." Her voice carried no emotion, "You'll never have to remind me of any clause."

Nisarg looked at her for a long moment before speaking, "There is one more thing." She stopped.

"Our families cannot know about this contract." She frowned slightly. "My parents will never agree if they believe this is just an arrangement."

He paused.

"So, for now... this stays between us."

Aadhya silently waited for him to continue.

"I'll send my parents to your house with a marriage proposal soon." Her fingers tightened around the file as he continued, "You should prepare your family."

She nodded once, "I'll tell them."

A strange silence settled between them.

Nisarg folded his arms across his chest, forcing the familiar arrogance back onto his face. It was easier to hide behind sarcasm than let her see the guilt threatening to consume him.

"Oh?" A faint smirk appeared on his lips, "And what exactly are you planning to tell them?"

His tone was teasing. Almost mocking.

"That a man you've barely spoken to in years suddenly wants to marry you?"

Aadhya slowly lifted her eyes to meet his. For a brief second, neither looked away. Then, in a calm voice, she finished the sentence he hadn't dared to.

"That we love each other."

The words hung between them, impossibly light... and unbearably heavy. Neither corrected them. Neither denied them. 

Because beneath the contract...

Beneath the pride...

Beneath years of misunderstandings...

It was the only honest sentence spoken in that office. Something unexpectedly settled inside Aadhya's heart. A quiet ache. She hated the circumstances. She hated the deal. Yet saying those words aloud felt strangely natural. As though somewhere, the girl she used to be had whispered them one last time.

Across the room, Nisarg remained perfectly still. His face betrayed nothing. Only he knew the chaos those four words had unleashed inside him.

_We love each other_.

He had spent years convincing himself otherwise. Years pretending those words belonged to another life. And yet, hearing them from her lips...

Even as a lie...

Felt dangerously close to the truth he had buried for so long. A smile almost escaped him. He swallowed it before she could see.

"Good." His voice returned to its professional calm, "Let's make sure everyone believes it."

Aadhya gave a quiet nod. She turned toward the door without another word. Neither of them realized that the biggest lie they were about to tell their families...

...was, in fact, the greatest truth they had never admitted to each other.

Her footsteps echoed across the quiet office. She opened the door. And nearly bumped into someone. Standing outside was a man in a perfectly tailored charcoal suit, one hand casually tucked into his trouser pocket.

Raghav Panchal.

The man every business magazine loved to call _the mind behind the Panchal empire._

His sharp eyes briefly met hers. She had seen him before. 

At media conferences. Award ceremonies.

She recognized him immediately. She quickly folded her hands, "Good afternoon, Mr. Panchal."

Raghav returned the greeting with a polite nod, "Good afternoon."

His gaze shifted to the file clutched tightly against her chest. Then to her swollen eyes. He didn't ask a single question.

Good businessmen observed first.

Questions came later.

Aadhya offered one final respectful nod before quietly walking away. 

Raghav stood there for a moment, watching the elevator doors close behind her. Something about her expression lingered in his mind.

Not fear. Not anger.

Heartbreak.

He turned and entered Nisarg's office. The moment he stepped inside, he sensed it. The room felt... different.

Too quiet. Too heavy.

Nisarg stood near the glass wall overlooking Ahmedabad, both hands resting in his pockets. He wasn't looking at the city. He was looking at the reflection of the elevator lobby in the glass. Watching until Aadhya completely disappeared from sight.

Raghav closed the door behind him, "So, I crossed paths with your client."

He walked closer.

"You've been staring out that window for quite some time."

Silence. That alone confirmed his suspicion.

He didn't need to ask what happened. He'd seen that look on Nisarg exactly once before, years ago, right before it disappeared for good. Raghav had watched Nisarg win impossible cases without losing composure. Today...

His shoulders looked heavier. His breathing slower. His silence louder.

"What did you do?" Another long pause. Finally...

Without taking his eyes off the window, Nisarg spoke. Barely above a whisper, "I made her sign it." 

Raghav's brows drew together, "The contract?" A slow nod.

"And..." Another pause. "I asked her to marry me."

For the first time in years, Raghav was genuinely speechless. He looked at his younger brother. Not as the famous lawyer. Not as the Panchal heir. But as the man hopelessly in love.

"So..." Raghav finally asked, "She agreed?"

Nisarg let out a humorless laugh, "She agreed to save her family." His words carried no victory. Only quiet guilt.

Raghav understood immediately. He walked over and stood beside his brother. 

Neither of them spoke for a while.

The city stretched endlessly beneath them.

Then Raghav looked sideways, "You know, if Mom finds out you finally agreed to marry..." A faint smile tugged at Nisarg's lips, "...she'll probably organize the wedding before sunset."

That earned the smallest huff of laughter. It disappeared almost instantly.

Raghav's expression softened, "You've loved her for years, Neil."

"Hmm."

"You also know she hates you right now."

"I know."

"And yet..." Nisarg finally looked at his elder brother. There was pain in his eyes. Hope too.

"I'd rather spend two years earning back her trust than spend a lifetime wondering what would've happened if I'd let her walk away again."

Raghav held his gaze for a long moment. Then, a knowing smile appeared on his face. He looked toward the door through which Aadhya had left only moments ago.

Before looking back at Nisarg. With complete certainty, he murmured, "She's yours. She always was."

The words echoed softly through the office.

Not as a claim.

But as an elder brother's quiet acknowledgment that fate had finally brought the right woman back into Nisarg's life.

Outside, the elevator doors closed.

Inside, two Panchal brothers stood silently, unaware that the deal signed that afternoon would one day become the strongest relationship either of them had ever witnessed.
