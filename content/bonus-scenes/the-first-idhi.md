The sound of keyboards filled the spacious cabin.

Raghav's office was unusually quiet for a Monday morning, some quarterly report staring at him. Floor to ceiling windows overlooked the city while sunlight spilled across the polished wooden floor. Everything inside the cabin had its own place. Files stacked with military precised location. Laptop exactly centered. Pen aligned parallel to the notebook. Coffee placed on the coaster without a single drop staining the desk.

And then...

There was Yug.

Completely ruining the symmetry.

He was sprawled across the long leather couch as if he owned the place, one leg hanging over the armrest, hoodie half zipped, laptop balanced dangerously on his stomach. One hand danced across the keyboard while the other shamelessly reached into a packet of lays masala chips every few seconds.

Crunch.

Type.

Crunch.

Type.

The boy somehow managed to code faster while eating.

Raghav looked up from a financial report for what felt like the hundredth time. Just gives him The Look. You know the one. Elder brother, eyebrow slightly raised, silence doing all the talking.

Yug sensed the stare without even looking. He slowly lifted his head. Their eyes met. Raghav didn't say a single word. He didn't have to.

Yug immediately straightened his back, pulled both feet off the couch, closed the chips packet halfway with the guilt of a man who was definitely not about to close it and sat like an obedient kindergarten student caught talking during assembly.

"Sorry. Bhai I was literally about to close it."

Raghav simply hummed before returning to his laptop. Exactly seven seconds passed. Yug survived seven whole seconds of silence. Then he dramatically sighed.

"Yug. Why are you even here."

"Because your office has actual light and doesn't smell like a courtroom, that's why."

"You have your own office. You can use spare cabin, even conference room. I suggest you to use pantry room to be precise."

"Neil bhai's office you mean. That place has more law books than my entire college library had. Every time I sit there I feel like I'm about to get sentenced for something." Yug shudders dramatically. "Files everywhere, only law books shelves, that one file cabinet that looks like it's been there since independence. It's giving depressing lawyer core, not giving productive workspace."

Raghav almost smiles. Almost.

"Also," Yug adds, not missing a beat, "my own office is under renovation. Getting the whole layout redone. New windows, better lighting setup, proper ergonomic chairs, the works. Full architectural revamp. So technically I have nowhere to sit."

"You've been saying that for two weeks."

"Renovations take time bhai, you can't rush art."

Raghav shakes his head and goes back to his screen, deciding it's not worth the argument. Yug goes back to typing, quiet for exactly four minutes, which for him is basically a personal record.

Peace had always been Yug's biggest enemy.

He resumed typing while casually asking, "Bhai..."

"Hm?"

"Why do you call bhabhi... Idhi?"

Raghav's fingers paused above the keyboard. His brows knitted together, "What?"

"Idhi. You call her idhi. Why."

"Why are you asking this randomly."

Yug shrugged without looking away from the screen, "My brain got stuck on that word."

Tap. Tap. Tap.

Yug shrugs, still typing, eyes on his screen like this is the most casual question in the world. "I don't know, my brain just landed on the word. Idhi. Then it went idhayan. Then I got curious." He finally looks up, grinning. "So? Story time?"

Tap.

"Because now I'm curious."

Raghav looked at him for a second before shaking his head, "You think too much."

"I know, Bhai."

Raghav doesn't answer right away. He just looks at his brother for a second too long, then turns back to his laptop like the conversation's over.

Silence settled again. Only this time... It wasn't silent inside Raghav's head. His eyes rested on the document before him, but the words slowly blurred.

The memory returned. Like it had happened yesterday.

✎﹏﹏﹏﹏

One month. It had already been one month since their marriage. It goes back a month into the marriage. A month of two people sharing a house and barely sharing a sentence. One month of sleeping under the same roof.

One month...

Yet they barely knew each other. They weren't strangers anymore. But they certainly weren't husband and wife in the emotional sense. Their conversations rarely crossed beyond necessities.

"Breakfast is ready."

"I'm leaving."

"Good night."

That was the extent of it.

When Sharda Baa insisted him to take Sreenidhi along for his France business trip, Raghav had silently agreed. Because apparently newlyweds are supposed to exist in the same time zone at least. So she went.

"A honeymoon doesn't always begin with romance," Baa had smiled knowingly. "Sometimes it begins with simply spending time together."

He hadn't understood her then.

The moment they landed in France, reality returned. His assistant, Sandeep, was already waiting with the itinerary, "Sir, the clients are expecting us."

Raghav turned toward Sreenidhi, "I'll have Sandeep drop you at the hotel first."

She simply nodded, "As you wish."

No complaints. No disappointment. Just another quiet nod. That was it. That was the extent of his attention that day.

By then she had already understood something about her husband. They hadn't built anything yet. No real conversations, no comfort, nothing beyond formal courtesy. And that day made it painfully clear to her that his business came before everything else, including her. She wasn't angry. Just... accepting.

The presidential suite overlooked the glowing streets of Paris. Anyone else would've spent hours admiring the city. Sreenidhi quietly unpacked her suitcase instead.

Hours later, Sandeep knocked, "Ma'am, sir asked me to bring you to the restaurant."

She quickly got ready. She barely had time to get ready, hair still slightly damp, nothing extravagant, just a simple elegant dress, with minimal makeup. Even after looking in the mirror three times, insecurity still lingered.

She had never attended an international business dinner before.

What if she embarrassed him?

What if she didn't fit into his world?

Taking a quiet breath, she followed Sandeep. The restaurant looked nothing less than royalty. Crystal chandeliers sparkled overhead. Soft piano music echoed through the hall. People conversed in fluent French while waiters moved like clockwork. She walked in feeling like she didn't belong in that room full of confident voices speaking over expensive wine.

And there... stood Raghav.

Confident. Sharp. Effortlessly commanding attention. 

He was greeting businessmen and businesswomen from different countries with practiced professionalism. The moment his eyes found her, he walked over.

Without hesitation, he placed himself beside her, "This is my wife, Sreenidhi Panchal."

One by one, he introduced her to every client. She politely smiled at everyone despite feeling completely out of place. 

Dinner began soon after. Menus were distributed. Everyone immediately started discussing dishes in French.

Sreenidhi stared at the menu like it was written in hieroglyphics, because it basically was. Every single word looked unfamiliar. Not in English. Not Hindi. Not Gujarati. Only French, no translation, no idea what half those words even meant. She couldn't understand a single line. She quietly flipped another page.

Same result.

Across from her, Raghav had already ordered his meal before diving straight back into merger discussions, market projections, numbers, investments, expansion and acquisitions. His attention remained completely occupied.

She looked down again. Maybe there would be pictures. There weren't. Her fingers tightened around the menu. She didn't want to interrupt him. Didn't want his clients thinking his wife couldn't even order food. When the waiter finally arrived...

She hesitated. Then softly pointed toward the only thing she recognized. Water. Salad, because that felt safe enough to guess. 

The waiter nodded. Minutes later every table was filled with beautiful dishes.

Steaming pasta.

Fresh breads.

Soups.

Seafood.

Desserts.

Only one plate looked painfully empty. A bowl of salad. One glass of water.

Raghav didn't notice. He was mid conversation about supply chains or contracts or something equally riveting, completely oblivious to the fact that his wife was sitting there with an empty plate basically.

One of Raghav's elderly clients noticed first. Glanced at her plate, just water and a small salad, and asked lightly if she was watching her diet.

"My dear," she smiled kindly, "only salad?"

She smiled, embarrassed, and said something about being on a diet. Anything but the truth, which was that she had no idea what to order and didn't want to admit it in a room full of strangers. It was easier than admitting...

I don't know your language.

I couldn't read the menu.

I didn't know what to order.

Raghav frowned at that. A random, disconnected thought crossed his mind. Diet? Why would she need to diet, she's small enough as it is. His eyes unconsciously drifted toward her. She was already so petite, so delicate and graceful. There wasn't even a little unnecessary weight on her. 

Why would she diet? The thought crossed his mind.

Then another business question pulled his attention away. He shrugged it off internally, filed it away as one of those things women just do, and went back to his clients.

The dinner ended. Meetings concluded. Handshakes exchanged. She barely touched her water glass more than a few times, and of course, Salad was all bland not upto her taste.

They returned to the hotel.

Inside the suite, both quietly freshened up, got ready for bed, and he said goodnight to her like it was any other night. Fell asleep almost instantly.

She didn't.

Her stomach growled loudly in the darkness. She slowly pressed a hand against it. Growling loud enough to embarrass her in the silence of that huge hotel room, and on top of that, her chest ached in a way that had nothing to do with hunger. It was that quiet kind of hurt. The kind where you realize the person next to you didn't even notice you were struggling.

Her eyes burned.

Not because of hunger alone. But because... He hadn't noticed. Not once. Not even accidentally. She knew he wasn't cruel. He was simply...

Oblivious.

Business occupied so much space in his mind and heart that the little details escaped him.

Around 2:15 in the morning, unable to bear the hunger anymore, she quietly slipped out of bed. She carefully walked toward the mini kitchen inside their presidential suite. Opening cabinets one after another. She searched hopefully. Most of the ready to eat meals contained meat. She closed each packet with a defeated sigh.

"I forgot..." She whispered to herself.

Then suddenly remembered.thank god, she'd packed some biscuits and snacks in her bag before the trip. Some habits from home die hard.

A tiny smile finally appeared.

She retrieved them, sat on the living room couch and slowly opened the packet, trying not to make any sound, eating biscuits as quietly as she could manage so she wouldn't wake him.

The silence of midnight accompanied every bite.

Outside, Paris glittered with lights.

Inside, a newly married girl quietly filled her stomach with biscuits because she didn't want to trouble her husband.

Back in the bedroom, Raghav shifted in his sleep. His hand instinctively reached toward the other side of the bed. Found the bed empty beside him, His eyes opened and sat up immediately.

Checked the balcony. Checked the bathroom. Checked the kitchen. A strange uneasiness settled inside him. Then...

He entered the living room. Finally found her in darkness, asleep sitting upright, curled sideways on the couch. An unopened biscuit packet rested beside her. Half eaten biscuits remained in her lap. Tiny crumbs decorated her cheeks and the corner of her lips.

He smiled because she looked ridiculously adorable. He stood there for a second just looking at her. Then his gaze landed on the empty wrappers. The untouched dinner replayed inside his mind.

Salad. Water. Diet.

Everything connected. Not dieting. She simply couldn't order. His chest tightened painfully.

"God..." The whisper escaped before he realized. And then it hit him. All of it, at once. How he forgot to make sure she ate. How he forgot she'd never once ask for anything, never demand, never complain, even while starving in a room full of strangers. How he forgot that ordering food in a foreign language might not be something she could just figure out on her own. How he'd spent the whole night talking business while his own wife sat there hungry and silent because she didn't want to embarrass either of them.

How had he not noticed?

How had he forgotten to ask what she wanted?

How had he assumed she'd manage everything herself?

Most importantly, how had he forgotten that his wife would rather stay hungry than inconvenience someone?

The realization hit harder than any business failure ever had. The guilt hit him like something physical. A weight settling right in his chest.

He slowly knelt before her sleeping figure, careful not to wake her, and gently wiped the crumbs from her cheek and chin with his thumb. And in that quiet dark room, in that guilt, something in him just cracked open a little.

She was a deep sleeper, thankfully, so she never stirred through any of it. He just knelt there a while longer, watching her breathe, feeling that strange mix of guilt and something softer he didn't have a name for yet.

Her peaceful sleeping face somehow made the guilt even worse.

"I'm sorry..." His voice barely existed, "So... so sorry..."

The apology lingered in the stillness. And then, without even meaning to, without even realizing the word was forming, "Idhi" slipped out of his mouth for the first time.

He froze. The name felt so warm, so soft. As though it had always belonged to her. As though his heart had known it long before his mind did. He looked at her again.

"Idhi..." This time the word carried something different.

Care. Affection. A quiet promise.

He gently tucked a loose strand of hair behind her ear, "I'll do better."

No grand declaration or no dramatic vow. Just one silent promise made to himself that night. That she'd come first from now on. Even if he never said it out loud. Even if he showed it in silence instead of words. Even if she never knew. He would remember.

That night quietly changed something inside Raghav Panchal. His wife wasn't simply Sreenidhi anymore. She had become...

His Idhi.

That night became the turning point. The night idhi entered his vocabulary and never left.

The next morning, while handing her a cup of tea without thinking, he casually said, "Idhi... your tea."

She blinked, "What?"

He looked equally confused, "Hm?"

"You called me... I... Idhi?" She said, clearly caught off guard.

He stared at her for a second before realizing what he'd said. He called her idhi like it was the most normal thing in the world.

"Oh..." A faint secret smile unknowingly appeared, "I did."

She looked completely baffled. But she never asked why. And he never explained. Just let this sweet moment sit there between them, this new small thing. The name simply stayed.

From that day...

Until today.

Raghav blinks, coming back to his office, back to the present, back to Yug staring at him expectantly like he's been waiting the entire time for an answer.

"...Bhai?" Yug's voice pulled him back. The office slowly returned. Raghav realized he had been smiling to himself. Yug had already stopped coding and was watching him with narrowed eyes.

"So?" Yug prompts.

Raghav leaned back in his chair. A rare softness settled across his otherwise unreadable face. His eyes unknowingly drifted toward their photograph resting on his desk.

"Saying 'Idhi'..." He smiled quietly, "...warms my heart in a way no one else ever could."

Yug listened silently. Just stares at him for a second, genuinely stunned his usually closed off brother just said something that soft out loud. Then he grins, quiet and knowing, the kind of smile that says he's definitely never letting this go but also not going to tease him about it right now.

Raghav's gaze never left the photograph.

"My heart feels like home every time I call her that." A small pause followed. Then he added, almost in a whisper, "Idhi... is my micasa."

Yug's eyebrows rose.

He had heard his elder brother say million dollar business deals with less emotion than those three words. A slow smile spread across Yug's face.

For once, Yug simply understood. Without another word, he returned to his coding. Raghav resumed reading his report. 

The office became quiet once again. Both brothers go back to their work. Yug's chips packet crinkles again a minute later. Raghav doesn't even bother giving him The Look this time.

Only this time...

The silence carried warmth.
