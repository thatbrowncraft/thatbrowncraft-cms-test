This is the closing verse of the chapter, and it feels like the whole teaching folding back into a single, quiet instruction. Jnana-chakshusha — the eye of knowledge. Not ordinary sight. A different kind of seeing, one that can finally tell apart the field, everything that's happening in you, from the knower, the one aware of it happening. I think a lot of suffering comes from a kind of mistaken identity — believing you are the anxious thought, or the bad mood, or the fear, rather than the one simply witnessing them pass through. This verse says liberation isn't about eliminating the field. Thoughts, feelings, changing circumstances will keep happening. It's about clearly seeing the difference between them and you. That distinction, once genuinely seen, does something to loosen the grip of whatever's currently loud in your mind. It's still there. But it's no longer the whole of you, holding you hostage. It becomes something happening in you, observed by something calmer, older, less shaken. I think this is why the chapter ends here, quietly, without fanfare. Not a dramatic conclusion. Just a small, precise seeing, offered gently, that changes how everything after it is experienced.
