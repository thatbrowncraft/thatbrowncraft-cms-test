What strikes me about this verse is the order of the three things — humility first, then questions, then service. Not the other way around. He's not saying serve so that you'll be rewarded with knowledge, like it's a transaction. He's saying come without pretending you already know, ask honestly, and let yourself be of use while you're learning. All three, together, held gently. I think a lot of us ask questions while secretly hoping to be told we were right all along. That's not really asking. That's just looking for agreement wearing the shape of a question. Real inquiry is a little more frightening than that, because it means you might hear something that rearranges what you believed. There's something very unguarded about pranipatena — the humbling of yourself before you even open your mouth to ask. Not humiliation. Just the willingness to not already have the answer. I think that's the real invitation here, more than the instruction to find a teacher. It's an invitation to stop performing certainty for a while. To let a question actually be a question. That's rarer than it sounds, and it's where real learning tends to begin.
