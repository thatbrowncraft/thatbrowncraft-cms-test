There's no shame built into this verse, if you actually read it closely. He doesn't say prevent the mind from wandering. He assumes it will. Yato yato — wherever, however often, it drifts. The instruction isn't about stopping the drifting. It's about the small, repeated act of noticing and returning. I think this changes what meditation, or any kind of discipline, actually is. It's not a state of never being distracted. It's the practice of coming back, over and over, without punishing yourself for having left. The coming back is the whole exercise. Not the staying. I find this quietly forgiving in a way that most instructions about focus aren't. Most advice about attention treats wandering as failure. This verse treats it as simply what minds do, the way water finds its way downhill. The discipline isn't in preventing the water from moving. It's in gently, patiently, redirecting it, as many times as it takes. So if your mind wandered fifty times today while trying to sit still, or focus, or pray, or just be present with someone you love — that's not fifty failures. That's fifty small returns home. He knew that would be the shape of it, and he still called it the practice.
