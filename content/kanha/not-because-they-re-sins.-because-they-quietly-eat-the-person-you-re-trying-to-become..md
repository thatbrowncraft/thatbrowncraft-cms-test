I've always found it interesting that this verse doesn't frame kama, krodha, and lobha — craving, anger, greed — as moral failings to be punished. It frames them as gates. Doors that, once walked through repeatedly, lead somewhere self-destructive, nashanam atmanah, the ruin of the self. That framing feels less like judgment and more like a warning from someone who cares about where you end up. Not 'you're bad for feeling these things,' but 'these particular doors, if you keep walking through them, tend to lead somewhere you don't actually want to go.' I think that distinction matters, because craving, anger, and greed are ordinary human experiences. Everyone feels them. The verse isn't asking for a person who never feels these things. It's asking for someone who notices the gate before walking through it, again and again, out of habit. I find this useful as a quiet check-in, especially in a moment of sudden anger or wanting. Not 'am I a bad person for feeling this,' but simply, 'is this a door I actually want to walk through right now.' That small pause, repeated often enough, is most of what this verse is really asking for.
