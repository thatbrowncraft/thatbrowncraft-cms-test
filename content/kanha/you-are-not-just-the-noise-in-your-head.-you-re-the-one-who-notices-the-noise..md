This chapter introduces a distinction that quietly changes how you might relate to your own mind — the field, kshetra, meaning the body and everything that happens within it, thoughts, moods, sensations, and the knower of the field, kshetrajna, the awareness that simply observes all of that happening. I think the relief in this verse is in realizing you're not obligated to identify completely with every thought or feeling that passes through you. The anxious thought this morning, the flash of anger an hour ago, the tiredness sitting in your shoulders right now — all of that is the field. It's happening in you. But it isn't the whole of who's doing the noticing. There's something steadying about that separation, especially on days when the mind feels chaotic, overrun, difficult to sit with. You don't have to fix the entire field before you can find some peace. You can simply notice that there's a quiet witness underneath the noise, one that was never actually shaken by any of it in the first place. That witnessing part, he says, is him too. Which means on the loudest days, there's still something in you that was never touched by the volume. Worth remembering it's there, even when it's hard to hear.
