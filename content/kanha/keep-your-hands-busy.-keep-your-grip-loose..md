There's a particular kind of tired that comes from clenching — holding onto a task so tightly that even finishing it doesn't bring relief, because you're already gripping the next thing before you've set the first one down. This verse is quietly asking for the opposite of that. Keep doing the work. Just stop strangling it. I notice, reading this again, that he doesn't say stop working, or work less, or rest more. He says work constantly — satatam. The instruction isn't to slow down. It's to loosen the grip while you keep moving. That's such a different ask than what most advice about burnout tends to say. I think that's because he's not diagnosing exhaustion as a scheduling problem. He's diagnosing it as an attachment problem. You can do less and still be clenched the whole time. You can do a lot and still feel light, if the grip isn't there. So maybe the question isn't how much you're doing today. It's whether your hands are open or closed around it. Open hands doing a lot can still feel like rest. Closed hands doing very little can still feel like drowning. He's pointing at the hands, not the hours.
