This verse is quietly radical, even now. He lists a respected scholar next to a cow, an elephant, a street dog, and someone from the lowest social rung imaginable in that time — and says the wise person sees all of them the same way. Not because their circumstances are the same. They're clearly not. But because something underneath all of them is exactly, precisely equal. I think this is one of those teachings that's easy to nod at and much harder to actually live. It's simple to say 'everyone has inherent worth.' It's much harder to feel that with the same warmth toward someone you've quietly ranked below you, even in your own mind, even without meaning to. Sama-darshinah — equal-seeing. Not equal-treating out of obligation. Equal-seeing, from somewhere honest inside. That's a much deeper ask than politeness. It's asking you to actually stop the ranking altogether, not just hide it better. I don't think anyone gets there completely. But I find it comforting that this is the definition of wisdom he chose — not how much someone knows, but how evenly they can see. That feels like a truer measure than most of the ones we usually use.
