Sulabhah — easy to reach. That word feels almost surprising in a sacred text. We tend to assume the divine must be difficult to access, guarded behind rituals, achievements, years of discipline before it opens even a little. But here he says plainly — for someone whose mind keeps returning to him, he's easy. Not distant. Not withheld. Easy. I think the actual difficulty isn't in reaching him. It's in the ananya-chetah part — the undivided mind. Not the effort of getting to him, but the effort of not constantly wandering everywhere else first. We're not far from him because he's hard to reach. We're far from him because our attention is scattered across a hundred other places, most of the time, without us even choosing it consciously. That reframes the whole practice, for me. It's not about striving harder toward something distant. It's about the much smaller, quieter act of turning attention back, again and again, toward something that was never actually far away to begin with. So if it feels like he's distant on a given day, this verse suggests otherwise. He's not the one who moved. Worth noticing, gently, where your own attention has been spending its time instead.
