I think this verse describes something quieter and rarer than most spiritual ideals do. Not the person who achieves the most, or feels the most intensely, or moves through the world dramatically. Just someone who neither disturbs others nor is easily disturbed by them. A kind of mutual steadiness, held gently in both directions. There's a phrase here I keep circling back to — harsha-amarsha-bhaya-udvegaih muktah — free from excessive elation, envy, fear, and anxiety. Not free from feeling entirely. Free from the excess of it, the part that spills over and disturbs everything nearby, including yourself. I think we sometimes admire intensity as though it's proof of how much someone cares. This verse quietly suggests something different — that the person who is dear to him isn't the most intense, but the most undisturbed. Not unfeeling. Just not thrown around by every gust of emotion that passes through. On a day when the world feels loud, when everyone around you seems to want a big reaction, this verse offers a different kind of permission. You don't have to match the volume. Staying steady, undisturbed, gently unbothered by the noise — that's not indifference. It might be exactly what's being called dear here.
