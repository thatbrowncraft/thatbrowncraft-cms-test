This verse is often talked about narrowly, as though it's only about the literal final moment of life. But I think it's quietly saying something bigger than that — that what naturally rises to the surface of your mind in a moment of total surrender is a reflection of what you've been feeding it, day after day, for years. You don't suddenly become peaceful in a crisis if you've never practiced peace before. You don't suddenly remember what matters in the hardest moment if you've spent your ordinary days completely absorbed in what doesn't. What shows up at the end tends to be whatever you rehearsed the most, quietly, without noticing you were rehearsing it. That's not meant to be frightening. It's meant to be an invitation, gently placed far ahead of you, to pay attention now to what you're practicing remembering — gratitude, love, this presence, this steadiness — long before any final moment arrives. I think of it less as a rule about dying well and more as a rule about living with intention. Whatever you keep coming back to today is quietly becoming the thing your mind will reach for automatically, when it matters most. That's worth choosing carefully, starting now, not later.
