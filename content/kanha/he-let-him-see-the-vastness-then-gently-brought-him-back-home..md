After the terrifying, overwhelming vision of the universal form — every world, every ending, all of time visible at once — Kanha doesn't leave Arjuna there. He returns. Saumya-vapuh — his gentle form, his familiar, human face, the one Arjuna actually recognized as his friend. I think this sequence matters as much as the vision itself. It would have been enough, dramatically, to end the chapter on awe and terror. But he doesn't. He deliberately steps back down into something smaller, softer, more bearable, because Arjuna needed comforting, not just revelation. There's something so tender in that choice. He was willing to be vast when the moment called for it, and just as willing to be small and familiar the moment his friend needed steadiness instead of scale. Both were real. Neither was a performance for the other. I think about this whenever something in life shows me how enormous and uncontrollable the world actually is — and then, afterward, I still need the ordinary, gentle things. A familiar voice. A quiet room. Someone's steady presence. This verse says that softness isn't a retreat from truth. It's part of the same care. He gave Arjuna both, in the order he needed them.
