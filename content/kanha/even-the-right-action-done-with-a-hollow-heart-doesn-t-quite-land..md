This closing verse of the chapter feels like a quiet warning against going through the motions. You can perform the correct action — give the right gift, say the right prayer, do the right thing — and still have it called asat, without real substance, if the faith behind it was hollow. I think this matters because it's tempting to believe that doing the right thing outwardly is enough on its own, regardless of what's happening inside while you do it. This verse says otherwise. The inner state — the shraddha, the genuine trust and sincerity behind the action — is what actually gives the action its weight. Without it, even a technically correct act is described as somehow empty. I notice this in smaller ways in my own life. An apology said without meaning it. A kindness done resentfully, just to check a box. The action looks the same from the outside, but something in it is missing, and I think people can usually feel that missing thing, even when they can't name it. So maybe this verse is less about doing more, and more about doing less half-heartedly. A smaller number of genuinely sincere acts might carry more real weight than a much longer list of hollow ones. Worth choosing quality of heart over quantity of gesture.
