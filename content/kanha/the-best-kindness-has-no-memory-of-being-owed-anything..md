Anupakarine — to one who cannot repay. That's the detail that makes this verse quietly demanding. It's easy to be generous toward someone who might return the favor someday, someone whose gratitude might matter later, whose goodwill is worth cultivating. This verse is describing something rarer — giving to someone with nothing to offer back, purely because giving was the right thing to do. I think this distinguishes real generosity from something that just looks like generosity from the outside. A lot of giving, if I'm honest, comes with quiet expectations attached, even unconsciously — a hope for recognition, gratitude, or reciprocity somewhere down the line. This verse is naming a different, cleaner kind of giving, one with no ledger running underneath it at all. I find this a useful, gentle check on my own motives sometimes. Would I still give this if I knew, for certain, nothing would ever come back to me — no thanks, no return favor, no one even noticing? If the answer is yes, that's probably closer to what this verse is describing. It's a high bar, honestly. But I think it's meant less as a test to pass perfectly and more as a direction to keep leaning toward, a little more, each time.
