I like reading this verse slowly, thinking of specific things while I do — a favorite song, a particular light at evening, the face of someone I love, the taste of something familiar cooked at home. This verse says, quietly, that all of it traces back to one origin. Not that these things are lesser copies of something better elsewhere. That they're genuine expressions of the same source, wearing different shapes. I find that changes how ordinary love feels. Loving a piece of music, or a person, or a particular kind of morning light isn't a distraction from something more spiritual. It might actually be one of the more direct ways this teaching reaches us — through the things we already, unmistakably, love. Bhava-samanvitah — worshipping with full feeling, not just correct ritual. That word matters here. It's not asking for technically accurate devotion. It's asking for whole-hearted devotion, the kind that shows up naturally when you love something without holding back. So maybe the invitation in this verse isn't to abandon the things you love in favor of something more abstract. It's to notice that loving them fully, honestly, without half-measures, was never separate from the larger thing to begin with.
