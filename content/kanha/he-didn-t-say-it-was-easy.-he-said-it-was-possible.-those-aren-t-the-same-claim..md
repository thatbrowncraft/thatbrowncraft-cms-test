Arjuna basically says, right before this, that controlling the mind feels as impossible as holding the wind. And Kanha doesn't argue with him. He agrees — asamshayam, without doubt, the mind is genuinely hard to control. He doesn't minimize the difficulty to make the teaching land easier. He validates it completely, and then says it's still possible anyway. I find that combination rare and important. So much comfort we're offered in hard moments tries to shrink the problem down to something manageable — it's not that bad, you're overreacting, it'll be fine. This verse doesn't do that. It says yes, this is exactly as hard as it feels. And also, it can still be done. Abhyasa and vairagya — practice and non-attachment — aren't presented as a quick fix. They're the long, unglamorous answer to a genuinely difficult problem. Not a trick. Not a hack. Just steady effort, repeated, alongside a loosening grip, over time. I think that's more honest than most self-improvement language, and somehow more comforting because of it. He's not promising it'll be easy. He's promising it's not hopeless. On a hard day, that's actually the more useful thing to hear.
