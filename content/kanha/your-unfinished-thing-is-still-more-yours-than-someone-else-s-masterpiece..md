I return to this one more than almost any other verse, quietly, on the days when I'm comparing myself to someone doing a completely different thing well. It's such a specific kind of ache — watching someone else's path look so much more finished than yours, so much more impressive, and forgetting that it was never supposed to be your path to begin with. He's not saying don't learn from others, or don't grow. He's saying something narrower and kinder: the imperfect version of the thing that's actually yours is worth more than a polished version of something that was never meant to be carried by you. That's not a consolation prize. That's the whole point. I think a lot of quiet unhappiness comes from people executing someone else's dharma really well and wondering why it doesn't feel like enough. It won't. It was never going to, no matter how well it's done, because it isn't the thing. So the invitation here isn't really about failure being fine, though it says that too. It's about permission — to keep walking your own uneven, imperfect road, instead of admiring someone else's smoother one from the sidelines.
