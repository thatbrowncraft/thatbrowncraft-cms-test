I read this less as a rule about religious texts specifically, and more as a practical piece of guidance for the moments when your own inner compass feels unreliable — clouded by anger, or craving, or exhaustion, exactly the states the previous verses were warning about. There's real wisdom in his suggestion here. He doesn't say 'trust your gut no matter what.' He says, when you can't trust your own judgment clearly, lean on something outside yourself that's proven steady — tradition, guidance, the accumulated wisdom of people who walked this road before you. I think this is a useful permission, especially for anyone who prides themselves on figuring everything out independently. Sometimes the wisest thing isn't inventing your own answer from scratch in a moment of confusion. It's borrowing a trustworthy map someone else already drew, following it carefully, until your own clarity returns. This doesn't mean abandoning your own judgment forever. It means recognizing that judgment isn't always reliable, especially in the grip of a strong craving or a flash of anger, and that there's no shame in leaning on something steadier in those moments. The map isn't a cage. It's a handrail, there for exactly the moments you need one.
