This one is quiet but it undoes something. We tend to imagine knowledge as arriving from outside — a book, a teacher, a moment of sudden clarity handed to us from somewhere else. But the second half of this verse says something different. It says the person who practices, in time, finds it within themselves. Not given. Found. Already there, waiting to be uncovered. I think that's a kinder way of understanding growth than the way we usually talk about it. It's not that you're empty and need to be filled up with the right ideas. It's that something in you is already whole, and the practice is mostly about clearing away what's been sitting on top of it. Kalena — in time — is doing a lot of work in this line too. Not instantly. Not because you read the right thing once. Because you kept showing up, and eventually what was already yours became visible to you. So if you feel like you're searching for something you can't quite name, this verse is a strange kind of comfort. You're not chasing something foreign. You're just waiting for the dust to settle on something that was already home.
