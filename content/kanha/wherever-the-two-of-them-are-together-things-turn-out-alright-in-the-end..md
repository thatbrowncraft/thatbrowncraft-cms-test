This is the final verse of the entire Gita, spoken not by Kanha but by Sanjaya, the narrator, closing out the whole conversation with a simple, quiet conviction — wherever Krishna and Arjuna stand together, wherever the divine and the willing human stand side by side, things settle into fortune, into rightness, in the end. I find it moving that the book doesn't end with Kanha's voice, giving one final instruction. It ends with someone simply watching the two of them together, and drawing a quiet conclusion from what he witnessed. Not a command. An observation, offered gently, after everything that came before it. I think that's a fitting close to eighteen chapters that began with a man collapsed in grief on his chariot, unable to lift his bow. The whole journey, from that trembling beginning to this final, steady line, was really about companionship — the human willing to keep showing up, however shakily, alongside the divine willing to keep walking beside him, however far the road went. So if you've made it through this courtyard, chapter by chapter, verse by verse, maybe that's the thing to carry out with you. Not a rule. Just this quiet observation. Wherever you keep showing up, and he keeps walking beside you, things tend to find their way to alright. Mati mama. That's his conviction. Not just Sanjaya's.
