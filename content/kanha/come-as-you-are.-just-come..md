There's no fine print in this verse. No conditions, no list of prerequisites, no mention of having to first become worthy. Just a simple, repeated set of instructions — think of me, love me, offer to me, bow to me — followed by a promise that feels almost startlingly certain. You will come to me. Not maybe. Evaishyasi — you will surely come. I think what moves me most is how direct and undramatic this is. It's not asking for perfection, or years of preparation, or a complete transformation before the invitation opens. It's asking for direction — where is your attention pointed — more than achievement. So many of us wait to feel ready before we turn toward something we love. We think we need to fix ourselves first, become someone more deserving, and then approach. This verse skips all of that waiting entirely. It simply says, turn your mind here, and that alone is the beginning of the whole thing. I read this as permission to stop waiting for the version of yourself you think would be more acceptable, and just turn toward him now, exactly as you are today. He didn't ask for anything else first. He just asked you to come.
