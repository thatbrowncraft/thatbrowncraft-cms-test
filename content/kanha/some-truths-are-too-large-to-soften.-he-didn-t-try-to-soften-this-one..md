This is one of the most unsettling lines in the entire Gita, and I think it deserves to stay unsettling rather than be smoothed over. He doesn't say 'I am gentle time, quietly moving things along.' He says he is time as destroyer — vast, unstoppable, already in motion, already gathering everyone toward an ending nobody can negotiate their way out of. I find something strangely honest, even comforting, in that refusal to soften it. So much of what we're told about difficulty tries to make it smaller than it is — it's not that bad, it'll pass quickly, don't worry. This verse doesn't do that. It looks directly at the scale of loss, of time, of endings, and says yes, this is exactly as vast and inevitable as it feels. I think there's a strange kind of relief in being told the truth plainly instead of being managed with comfort. It means your fear of loss, of time passing, of things ending, was never an overreaction. It was an accurate reading of something genuinely enormous. He shows Arjuna this terrifying vastness not to frighten him into obedience, but because pretending it wasn't there would have been its own kind of dishonesty. Sometimes the kindest thing is simply not to lie about how big something is.
