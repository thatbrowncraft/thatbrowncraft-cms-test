This is the closing verse of the chapter on devotion, and I think it works as a kind of quiet summary of everything that came before it. Not a demand for perfection. A recognition of continued faith — shraddadhana, full of trust — walked steadily, over time, toward him as the highest thing. I notice it doesn't say 'those who never falter' or 'those who master it completely.' It says those who follow it, with faith, as taught. That's a much gentler bar than mastery. It's asking for persistence and sincerity, not flawlessness. I think that's worth remembering on the days when devotion, or discipline, or any long practice feels unsteady — when you missed a day, or your attention wandered, or you felt further from the goal than you did last week. This verse isn't measuring you against perfect execution. It's noticing the faith itself, the fact that you kept turning back toward it, even imperfectly. Ativa me priyah — exceedingly dear to me. Not conditionally dear, pending better performance. Simply dear, because the trying itself, sustained honestly over time, was always the real offering. He seems to have known that all along.
