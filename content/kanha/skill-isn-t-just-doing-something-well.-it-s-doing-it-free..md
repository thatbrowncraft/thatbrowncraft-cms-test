'Yoga is skill in action' is such an unusual definition, if you think about it. We usually think of skill as technical — how precisely you do a thing. But he's pointing at something else. The real skill, he's saying, is in how lightly you hold the doing. Not careless. Light. There's a kind of person who can do something extremely well and still be miserable the whole time, gripping every step, terrified of the outcome. And there's another kind who does the same thing, just as carefully, but without that white-knuckled grip. He's saying the second one has actually mastered something the first hasn't — not the task, but the relationship to the task. I think about this whenever I catch myself doing something 'right' but with my jaw clenched the entire time. That's not mastery yet, even if the result looks fine from the outside. Mastery, in his sense, includes the ease. It's a quiet redefinition of what it means to be good at something. Not just precise. Also unburdened. That's a much harder thing to practice than technique, and I think that's exactly why he called it yoga — a discipline, not a talent you're born with.
