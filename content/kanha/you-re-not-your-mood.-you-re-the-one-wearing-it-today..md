This chapter introduces the three gunas — sattva, clarity and calm; rajas, restlessness and drive; tamas, heaviness and inertia — as forces that move through everyone, shifting and mixing constantly, shaping how a day feels from the inside. And right at the start, he says these bind the deha, the body, but not the dehin, the imperishable soul within it. I find that distinction quietly steadying. It means the heaviness you're feeling today, or the restless agitation, or even the calm clarity on a good day — none of it is actually you at the core. It's a quality passing through you, the way weather passes through a season, changing again tomorrow, next week, next mood. I think this matters most on the tamasic days — the sluggish, low, unmotivated ones that can feel like evidence of some deeper failing. This verse suggests otherwise. It's not a verdict on your worth. It's just a guna, doing what gunas do, moving through for a while before something else moves through in its place. So on a heavy day, it might help to think of it less as 'I am like this' and more as 'this quality is visiting today.' Visitors leave. What they visited was never actually them to begin with.
