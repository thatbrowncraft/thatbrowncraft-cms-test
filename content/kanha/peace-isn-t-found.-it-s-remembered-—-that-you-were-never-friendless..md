Suhrid — friend. Of every being, not just the devoted ones, not just the ones who got it right. That word is doing something very tender in this verse. He doesn't call himself the judge, or the accountant, or the one keeping the ledger of merit. He calls himself the friend. And peace, he says, comes from knowing that. I think we tend to look for peace by trying to fix everything that's unsettled — solve the problem, resolve the conflict, get the outcome we want. And sometimes that works, for a while. But this verse is pointing somewhere else entirely. It's saying peace comes from remembering a relationship, not from resolving a situation. There's something in that I find quietly moving. That underneath every difficult day, every mess you haven't sorted out yet, there's a friendship that was never contingent on you sorting it out first. Not earned through good behavior. Just there, the way a good friend is there, uninvited, before you even ask. Maybe that's why he places shanti — peace — right after suhrid in this line. Not after victory. After being reminded you were never alone in the first place.
