Sarva-vid — knows everything. That's a striking claim to make about knowing just one thing clearly. Not knowing every fact, every answer, every detail about how the world works. Just knowing him, purushottama, clearly, without confusion, and somehow that one seeing is described as containing all the rest. I think this points at something I've noticed quietly in my own life — that some kinds of understanding don't come from accumulating more information. They come from one thing becoming clear enough that everything else reorganizes itself around it. You don't need every question answered first. You need one true thing, held steadily, and the rest starts to make more sense in its light. I find this comforting on the days when there's so much I don't understand — about grief, about timing, about why some things happen the way they do. This verse doesn't promise those questions will all get answered. It suggests something gentler: that knowing him clearly is itself a kind of completeness, even while so much else remains unresolved. So maybe the invitation isn't to solve everything before turning toward him. It's to turn toward him first, clearly, sarva-bhavena, with your whole being, and let the rest follow at its own pace.
