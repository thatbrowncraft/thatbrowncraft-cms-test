He just... sits down. In the middle of the battlefield, with everyone waiting, the great warrior lowers his weapons and lets his mind fall apart. Nobody stops him. Nobody rushes him back up. The chariot just stays there, still, while a strong man grieves in front of everyone he was supposed to lead. I think about how rare that is to witness — someone allowed to simply stop, without being told to hurry up and pull it together. The next seventeen chapters exist because Arjuna gave himself permission to sit down first. If he'd forced himself to keep standing, to keep performing readiness he didn't feel, none of the conversation that follows would have happened. Sometimes putting the bow down isn't giving up. It's just being honest that you can't hold it right now, and that honesty is what makes room for something wiser to reach you. If you're at a point where you need to sit down in your own version of this field, let yourself. The most important conversation of your life might be waiting exactly there, for exactly this version of you — the tired one, not the composed one.
