This might be the most quoted line in the whole Gita, and I think it gets flattened sometimes into just 'don't worry about outcomes,' which isn't quite it. It's more specific than that. He's saying your relationship with the work itself should be complete on its own — not a transaction where you're constantly checking if the universe has paid you back yet. That's such a relief, actually, once you sit with it. Because so much of our exhaustion doesn't come from the work. It comes from refreshing the results. Checking if it landed. Measuring whether it was worth it yet, before it's even finished happening. He's not telling you to stop caring. He's telling you where to put the caring — into the doing, not into the waiting. The waiting will happen regardless of how tightly you grip it. I think this is why he also warns against attachment to inaction, in the same breath. It's not about doing less. It's about doing fully, and then actually letting your hands open. Both halves matter. Show up completely. Let go completely. That's the whole practice, in one breath, given to someone who badly needed to hear it.
