There's a repetition in the final line of this verse that I find almost startling in its plainness — yah pashyati sa pashyati. 'One who sees, sees.' As though seeing this properly is so complete, so total, that there's nothing more to add. You either see it, or you haven't yet. What he's describing is a way of looking at things that holds two truths at once — that everything around us is constantly changing, aging, shifting, ending, and that underneath all of it, something remains completely unmoved. Not despite the change. Right in the middle of it, equally present in every changing thing. I think about this on the days when everything feels like it's slipping — relationships evolving, seasons of life ending, people changing, myself changing in ways I can't always track. This verse doesn't deny any of that motion. It just says there's something steady woven through it, present in equal measure in every shifting form, never actually going anywhere. I don't think you arrive at seeing this all at once. It's more like a practice of noticing, again and again, the stillness underneath the motion, until eventually it becomes the thing you see first, instead of last.
